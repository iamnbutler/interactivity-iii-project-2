Welcome

This project is an exploration into sorting. The objects in the gallery were found while walking around the York University campus and were sorted into grades by viability as a construction material. Materials were taken less literally, and often represent a group of materials.

Close

  • Glass

    Grade A (1/5)

    A
    Glass is a rather popular building material. Offering both transparent and translucent options, it can be used for a variety of purposes, from walls to windows to roofs.

  • Metal

    Grade A (2/5)

    A
    Metal is the quintessential building material of the modern era. It enables high rise apartments, high speed train rails, and much more. In 1855 Sir Henry Bessemer invented the process for creating steel, the primary metal used in construction.

  • Rock

    Grade A (3/5)

    A
    Rock is one of the oldest building materials. Used back as far as history stretches, rock and stone have always been involved in construction, and many of the worldâ€™s wonders are constructed (or naturally occur) from rock.

  • Bolt

    Grade A (4/5)

    A
    Large bolts, ranging anywhere from an inch to a foot or more, hold together many of the structures that we use on a daily basis. Combined with steel supports and concrete it is a primary building material.

  • Wood

    Grade A (5/5)

    A
    Wood is the last of the A grade building materials in this collection. It is used heavily in residential construction, and in all forms of decoration, furniture and appliances.

  • Battery

    Grade B (1/6)

    B
    While not used as the building material, batteries and electricity are extremely important to construction. It powers lighting, high powered machines and tools that workers use daily.

  • Bamboo

    Grade B (2/5)

    B
    Bamboo is a great scaffolding material. In Asian countries it is used to build scaffold around buildings while they are constructed. It is also a sturdy material used for any form of temporary construction.

  • Light

    Grade B (3/5)

    B
    Light is very important to construction. It is obviously not a construction material per se but it provides the ability to work at night and in dark areas, as well as provide safety for workers, letting each other know where they are.

  • Tape & Ribbons

    Grade B (4/5)

    B
    While on their own they do not have much use as construction materials, tape + ribbons take more of a support role, warding off areas and bundling things together.

  • Screw

    Grade B (5/5)

    B
    Screw are great for holding small things together. Great for models and furniture, but used less in large scale construction.

  • Plastic Utensils

    Grade C (1/6)

    C
    Plastic utensils, while not designed as a building materials, are at least rigid, and used in enough quantity, could be used for temporary construction. Possibly viable for construction of small models.

  • Pinecone
